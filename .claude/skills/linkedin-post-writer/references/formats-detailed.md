# LinkedIn Post Formats - Detailed Worked Examples

One complete worked example per format. Each section has:

- **Template** (fill-in-the-blank scaffold)
- **Worked example** (finished post)
- **Commentary** (why it works against the 2026 algorithm and human attention)

Use these as reference when drafting. Match the structure, not the words.

---

## 1. POV / Contrarian

### Template

```
"[Common piece of advice]" is [strong dismissal].

Here's what I've actually seen after [credibility marker: N years / N clients / N projects]:

[Concrete counter-evidence in 2-3 short paragraphs.]

The real issue isn't [what everyone blames]. It's [the reframe].

[One-line summary of the better mental model.]

[Question that invites disagreement.]

#[hashtag] #[hashtag] #[hashtag]
```

### Worked example

> "Quiet quitting" is the most misused phrase in business media.
>
> I've run hiring for 6 companies over 12 years. What gets labeled "quiet quitting" is almost never the employee's problem.
>
> It's a manager who stopped giving feedback 8 months ago. A scope that quietly tripled without a title change. A promotion cycle that keeps slipping.
>
> Employees don't quiet quit. They quietly disengage from leadership that stopped paying attention.
>
> If half your team looks checked out, the problem isn't the team.
>
> What's the worst "quiet quitting" misdiagnosis you've seen?
>
> #Leadership #Management #PeopleOps

### Commentary

- **Hook earns the expand.** Line 1 attacks a phrase the reader has definitely seen this week. It's 68 characters, well under the 210-char cutoff.
- **Credibility marker in line 3** ("6 companies over 12 years") buys the right to disagree.
- **Concrete examples** (feedback, scope, promotions) beat abstractions ("engagement"). Readers recognize themselves.
- **Reframe is quotable.** "They quietly disengage from leadership that stopped paying attention" is the line that gets screenshotted.
- **CTA invites war stories.** Commenters want to vent about managers; the question gives them permission.
- **3 hashtags, no links, short paragraphs.** Algorithm-clean.

## 2. Story

### Template

```
[Specific, sensory opening: time + place + action in past tense.]

[Tension: what was at stake, in 2-3 short paragraphs.]

[Turning point: the exact moment something shifted.]

[Aftermath: the before/after in one or two lines.]

[Lesson: one sentence, plain language, generalizable.]

[Question that invites the reader's version.]

#[hashtag] #[hashtag] #[hashtag]
```

### Worked example

> On a Thursday in March 2021, I walked into a glass conference room to tell my best employee I had to let her go.
>
> Runway was 4 months. The board had forced a 30% cut the night before. I'd rehearsed the words in the car. None of them came out right.
>
> She listened. Then she said: "I know. I've been watching the numbers too. How can I help you land the next round?"
>
> She stayed on as a contractor for 6 weeks for free, wrote the case study that closed our Series A, and is now a GM at a company I introduced her to.
>
> I used to think loyalty meant staying. I learned it means showing up - even on the way out.
>
> Who's someone who showed up for you when they didn't have to?
>
> #Leadership #Startups #Culture

### Commentary

- **Specificity is the hook.** "Thursday in March 2021," "glass conference room," "4 months runway" - the reader pictures it instantly.
- **Past tense + concrete nouns** outperform present-tense reflection. Stories are verbs.
- **The dialogue line is the turning point.** One quoted sentence from the other person makes the whole post feel real, not performative.
- **Lesson is 1 sentence.** The worst story posts have 4-paragraph lessons. Trust the reader.
- **CTA asks for a story back.** Story-for-story is the highest comment-depth pattern on LinkedIn.
- **Length ~1,100 chars.** Right in the story sweet spot (can stretch to 1,800 if the middle earns it).

## 3. Listicle

### Template

```
[Bold, numbered claim. Ideally a count in the first 6 words.]

[One line of context or credibility.]

1. [Item - 1 line, ideally with a twist or specific]
2. [Item]
3. [Item]
4. [Item]
5. [Item]
[...up to 7 items max]

[One-line summary that reframes the whole list.]

[Question: which one resonates, which would they add?]

#[hashtag] #[hashtag] #[hashtag]
```

### Worked example

> 6 things I stopped doing that made me a better founder:
>
> After 9 years running venture-backed companies, these were the highest-ROI subtractions:
>
> 1. Checking email before 10am.
> 2. Taking "quick intro calls" with zero agenda.
> 3. Reviewing every draft before it shipped.
> 4. Running 1:1s as status updates.
> 5. Using Slack during deep work blocks.
> 6. Saying yes to panels.
>
> Every time I subtracted something, a better use of the hour showed up.
>
> What's something you stopped doing that made you better at your job?
>
> #Founders #Productivity #Leadership

### Commentary

- **Number in the first 3 words.** "6 things" signals a scannable payoff.
- **"Stopped doing" is counterintuitive.** Most listicles are additive ("7 tools you need"). Subtraction lists get saved at 2x the rate.
- **Items are specific verbs, not concepts.** "Checking email before 10am" beats "Respecting deep work." Specificity is shareable.
- **One line per item.** Anything longer and the list stops feeling like a list.
- **Summary line is a small philosophical gift.** "Every time I subtracted something, a better use of the hour showed up" is the save-worthy payoff.
- **CTA is low-cost to answer.** "What did you stop doing?" is easier than "What do you do?" - easier = more comments.

## 4. Case Study

### Template

```
[Outcome in numbers, as the hook. Before -> after if possible.]

The problem: [1-2 sentence situation with a metric].

What we did:
- [Concrete action 1]
- [Concrete action 2]
- [Concrete action 3]
- [Concrete action 4]
- [Concrete action 5 - max 5 bullets]

The result: [hard numbers, before vs after, with a timeframe].

The takeaway: [one-sentence transferable principle].

[Question: where else could this apply?]

#[hashtag] #[hashtag] #[hashtag]
```

### Worked example

> We took a B2B SaaS from 0.4% to 0.9% demo conversion in 42 days - no added headcount, no paid ads.
>
> The problem: 12k monthly visitors, flat pipeline for 3 quarters. The CMO was 30 days from being replaced.
>
> What we did:
> - Rewrote the hero to lead with outcome, not feature.
> - Collapsed 3 pricing tiers into 2.
> - Added a 60-second product tour above the fold.
> - Moved social proof from footer to hero.
> - Retargeted bounced traffic with a case study ad.
>
> The result: demo conversion went 0.4% -> 0.9%. $180k in new pipeline in 6 weeks. CMO kept her job.
>
> The takeaway: most conversion problems are clarity problems, not traffic problems.
>
> Where are you buying more traffic when you should be fixing the page?
>
> #GrowthMarketing #B2BSaaS #ConversionRate

### Commentary

- **Lead with the number.** Hook is the outcome, not the setup. "0.4% to 0.9% in 42 days" is specific enough to be believable.
- **Human stakes in the problem.** "CMO was 30 days from being replaced" - stakes make the case study memorable, not just instructive.
- **Actions are verbs, not platitudes.** "Rewrote the hero" beats "Optimized messaging." Readers should be able to copy the move.
- **5 bullets max.** More and the reader stops reading; less and it feels thin.
- **Takeaway is the punchline.** "Clarity problems, not traffic problems" is the line the reader screenshots.
- **CTA is pointed.** "Where are you buying more traffic when you should be fixing the page?" implicates the reader - highest-engagement CTA pattern.

## 5. Observation / Pattern

### Template

```
I've [noticed/seen/tracked] [specific pattern] across [N] [credibility-building unit: clients / companies / candidates / deals].

[The pattern in 1-2 sentences.]

[Evidence: 2-3 concrete signals or examples.]

[Why it happens: the deeper insight.]

[Why it matters: the second-order consequence for the reader.]

[Question: have they seen it too?]

#[hashtag] #[hashtag] #[hashtag]
```

### Worked example

> I've reviewed 40+ seed-stage pitch decks this quarter.
>
> The funded ones all share one trait: slide 2 is about the customer, not the product.
>
> The unfunded ones open with "what we built."
> The funded ones open with "who we serve and what hurts."
> Same market. Same traction. Different story order.
>
> Why it works: investors don't underwrite products. They underwrite a founder's obsession with a specific pain.
>
> Why it matters: if your deck opens with your product, you're telling the investor you're more interested in the thing you made than the people you made it for.
>
> If you're raising right now - where does your deck put the customer?
>
> #VentureCapital #Startups #Fundraising

### Commentary

- **Hook is a credibility flex + curiosity gap.** "40+ decks this quarter" earns the right to generalize. "One trait" teases the payoff.
- **Parallel construction in the evidence.** "The unfunded ones... The funded ones..." is the rhetorical engine - it makes the pattern feel inevitable.
- **Two "why" beats.** Why it happens (insight) + why it matters (stakes). Most observation posts skip the second; it's the half that drives the save.
- **Implicating CTA.** "If you're raising right now..." targets the exact reader who needs the post, which drives both comments and DMs.
- **No jargon.** "Underwrite a founder's obsession with a specific pain" is dense but plain-English. Observation posts fail when they hide behind consultant-speak.
- **Length ~1,000 chars.** Tight. Observation posts lose power if they wander.

## Cross-format principles

These show up in every worked example above - make sure your draft has them:

1. **Specificity beats polish.** Named years, named dollar amounts, named job titles. "40+ decks" > "many decks."
2. **One quotable line per post.** The line a reader would screenshot. Put it near the end.
3. **Paragraphs are 1-2 lines.** If a paragraph is 3+ lines on desktop, it's 6+ on mobile. Break it.
4. **CTAs implicate the reader.** "Where are you...?" beats "What do you think?" The former gets answers; the latter gets crickets.
5. **Hashtags last, 3-5, no link in body.** Every time.
